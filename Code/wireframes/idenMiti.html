Identification and Mitigation
Read on how to identify phishing messages and the mitigation techniques you should take.

What is Phishing?

Phishing is a particular type of various social engineering attacks where cybercriminals deceive unexpecting victims into handing over sensitive information which can include credit card details and account credentials. Installation of malware also falls under the social engineering method.

How Phishing Works

As complicated as phishing may seem, cybercriminals commonly only use one of two basic methods:

Malicious attachments

If victim has received a phishing email or message, there is a chance that it came with a malicious attachment. It is common for these attachments to have enticing names such as "INVOICE" or "IMPORTANT!" which increases the probability of the victim opening the email or message.

Malicious links

Malicious links can be sent to victims masquerading as safe and legitimate websites. These malicious website often contain scripts that stores user credentials without the victim knowing. Information taken by the script will then be available to the cybercriminals.

The Common 5

There are many types of phishing which cybercriminals use on victims in order to gain valuable information and credentials. The following 5 are the most common types of phishing that are used on people.

Email phishing

Most phishing attacks are done through email phishing. Email phishing is where cybercriminals will register a domain that looks like a real, genuine company or organisation. The cybercriminals will then send a generic email to thousands of people hoping to get a response containing information that they have asked for. Creating a fake domain is simple but yet effective. It can often involve character substitution like using an lowercase ‘L’ in place of an uppercase ‘i’ which look very identical (l & I).

Spear phishing

Spear phisihing is a more sophisticated type of phishing involving email. Spear phishing is where malicious emails are sent to a specific person. Cybercriminals will most likely know some information about the victim. The information can include but not limited to:

•Place of employment;
•Job title;
•Email address; and
•Specific information about their job role.



Whaling

Whaling attacks are even more targeted compared to Spear phishing, taking aim towards Senior Executives of companies. Although the main objective of Whaling is the same as any other types of phishing, the technique used is a lot subtler.

Using fake links and malicious URLs are not useful as cybercriminals attempt to imitate senior staff of an organisation.

Whaling emails also commonly use the pretext of a busy CEO who wants an employee to do them a favour.



Smishing and Vishing

Smishing and Vishing replaces emails with telephones/mobile phones as the form of communication. Smishing involves criminals sending text messages (of content more or less the same that of email phishing), and Vishing involves a telephone conversation.

One of the most common smishing pretexts are messages supposedly from your bank alerting you to suspicious activity or your mobile phone network asking you to pay an additional fee whether or not you are registered with said bank or network.

Angler phishing

Angler phishing is a relatively new phishing vector. Social media offers a number of ways cybercriminals can trick people and the user of the platform through the use of fake URLs; cloned websites, posts, and tweets; instant messaging (for example Instagram Direct Messages). These can be used to persuade users and people to disclose sensitive information or even download malware.

5 Ways to Identify Phishing

There are many ways in spotting if an email or message is illegitimate. Here are 5 common ways to spot them. Remember, there are many more ways to figure them out. The following 5 are the most common ways to.

1. Demanding Urgent Action

Emails threatening negative consequences, or a loss of opportunity unless you take action, are often phishing emails. Cybercriminals will use this approach to rush victims into action before they figure out that the email they were sent is illegitimate through flaws and inconsistencies.

2. Bad Grammar and Spelling Mistakes

Bad grammar and spelling mistakes is often a big red flag in phishing emails and messages. The companies criminals are trying to imitate are unlikely to have any spelling errors or have bad grammar in the messages they send to their clients. Seeing bad grammar and spelling errors in emails and messages can and will mean that it is of malicious intent.

3. Requesting Login Credentials, Payment Information or Sensitive Data

Emails and messages from an unexpected or unfamiliar sender that request login credentials, bank details or other sensitive information should always be treated with caution before making a move. Cybercriminals are able to forge login pages to look legitimate and send an email containing a link to the malicious page. As a recipient, you should refrain from clicking and inputting information on such forms unless you are 100% sure that it came from a legitimate sender and that the link is not deceptive in any way, shape or form.

4. Suspicious Attachments

Work-related file sharing now takes place either through internal networks of a company or through third-party services such as OneDrive or Dropbox. Emails with attachment should always be treated suspiciously especially if they have an unfamiliar extension such as .zip,.exe, etc.

5. Too Good to Be True Emails and Messages

Too good to be true emails and messages are those which incentivize the victim to click on a link, open an attachment, or reply back. By doing so, they will be rewarded in some way either through money, vouchers or even trips abroad.

Mitigating Phishing

Think before you click

You should always think about what may happen when you receive an email. Did you expect it? Is it too good to be true? Logos and names are often replicated and you should not always rely on it.

Be weary of the requests the email may be asking

You may use email as a form of communication method for your work and requests such as what time a meeting will be are fine, more often than not. So if an email is asking for your login credentials and bank bank details, be weary.



Learn the psychological triggers

All social engineering attacks exploit human psychology to get past victims’ natural wariness, such as:

•Creating a false sense of urgency and heightened emotion to confuse their victims;

•Exploiting the human propensity for reciprocation by creating a sense of indebtedness; or

•Relying on conditioned responses to authority by seeming to issue orders from senior figures.



SSL Certificate

Before putting any details in a website, make sure to check for the SSL certificate. Secure Sockets Layer (SSL) Certificate ensures that all data passed from the browser to the website's server is secure. Don't give information to an unsecured site.



Change passwords regularly and immediately

You should change your passwords regularly which differs from each service to service and site to site. You should also change your passwords immediately if you have any doubt that your account has been compromised.



Firewalls are your friends

Firewalls are very effective in preventing external attacks. Firewalls acts as a shield between your computer and an attacker. Some firewalls can also be installed in your browser which can help with phishing emails.
